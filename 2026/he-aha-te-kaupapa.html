Whakaaro

He aha te kaupapa o tēnei rangitaki?

He wāhi tēnei mō ngā kōrero e pā ana ki te hangarau, te pūkaha, me te ao Māori.

This is a place for discussion about technology, engineering, and the Māori world. It is my corner of the web to learn and explore topics of interest to me that relate to modern technology, engineering practices and te ao Māori. In the process I want to explore how tangata whenua can benefit from current and emerging technology through kaupapa Māori.

Ko wai ahau?

Who am I?

He uri ahau nō Ngāti Pikiao, Ngāti Whakaue, Ngāti Porou. Nō Rotorua ahau. I was born and raised in Rotorua. Spent some time in Tauranga and Wellington before returning to my papa kāinga in Rotorua around 6 years ago.

I don't have any social media accounts. I try to keep a low-profile (especially online). I don't really like attention and I value privacy. I'm not keen to have my life on display for strangers.

So why write a public blog? Isn't this a bit out there for someone like me? The main reason is because I keep losing track of ideas and thoughts and now I want a place where I can record them. I could create an offline document and record my thoughts that way but I figure someone else may find the whakaaro helpful or interesting. If not, kei te pai.

Most importantly this blog is a personal diary and a place where I can extract and unravel my thoughts on topics that are either important and/or interesting to me. It gives me a medium where I can bring order and clarity to my thoughts on modern technology and te ao Māori. A set of documents I can access anywhere in the world on any device that is capable of running a web browser.

Te Pae Tukutuku o te Ao

He tamaiti ahau nō ngā tau iwa tekau (I'm a child of the 90's).

I remember when I first played Super Mario Brothers and Duck Hunt on Nintendo. Alex the Kid and Sonic the Hedgehog on the Megadrive. Street Fighter 2 arcade battles at the local dairy. Playing Duke Nukem 3D and Quake capture the flag over 28.8kbps. Getting Final Fantasy VII for christmas and clocking up over 100 hours on it.

I was born in the 80's but grew up in the 90's. It was a sweet decade to grow up in, especially being around during the early days of gaming. I've got tons of fond memories from this period in my life.

But the best part was when I first got access to the internet and the World Wide Web.

Experience of connecting to the web, why it is important and how it lead to my career in software and web development.

Kaihanga pūmanawa rorohiko

I've been working professionally as a software developer for the past 14 years. Mostly on the web and most of the time leaning towards the frontend of the stack while also tinkering with backend services, containers and infrastructure from time to time. But my main focus has been on UX engineering. Developing performant, accessible and intuitive user experiences.

I try to approach my work from the mindset of a systems thinker, keeping in mind all of the interconnected systems in the process. I like to understand how everything under the hood works, how it all connects together and how to optimise the system.

I'm a problem solver and tinkerer and I've always been curious about how things work at a deep level. I find steep learning curves and deep layers of complexity engaging and fun. The harder the problem, the more satisfying it is to find elegant solutions to the problems. Making sense of complex problems and searching for elegant solutions keeps me engaged and curious.

This is where the craft in software development comes in. I view software development through the lens of a craftsperson. Like a sculptor drawing out form from raw materials, software developers draw out solutions from complexity (and, at times, attempt to bring order to chaos).

The experience of taking complex problems and simplifying the complexity is kind of like tuning the frequency reception of a VHF/UHF band TV. Tuning the reception until it is within the frequency range of a TV channel. Then proceeding to improve the picture by tweaking the tune to get as close as possible to the optimum frequency. Now the picture is as clear as we can get it. The environment (physical location, antenna and TV) can also dictate the quality of the reception and picture quality. It's a process and experience of moving from ambiguity to clarity. In my opinion this is the space where elegant solutions emerge.
