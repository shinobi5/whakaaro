Whakaaro

He aha te kaupapa o tēnei rangitaki?

He wāhi tēnei mō ngā kōrero e pā ana ki te hangarau, te pūkaha, me te ao Māori.

This is a place for discussion about technology, engineering, and the Māori world. It is my corner of the web to learn and explore topics of interest to me that relate to modern technology, engineering practices and te ao Māori. In the process I want to explore how tangata whenua can benefit from current and emerging technology in a kaupapa Māori directed way.

Ko wai ahau?

Who am I?

He uri tēnei nō Ngāti Pikiao, Ngāti Whakaue, Ngāti Porou. Nō Rotorua ahau. I was born and raised in Rotorua. Spent some time in Tauranga and Wellington before returning to my papa kāinga in Rotorua around 6 years ago.

I don't have any social media accounts. I try to keep a low-profile (especially online). It suits me. I don't really like attention. I value privacy and I'm not keen to have my life on display for strangers.

So why write a publicly available blog? Isn't it a bit out there for someone like me? The main reason is because I keep losing ideas and thoughts to memory. I want to record them. I could create an offline document and record my thoughts that way. But I figured someone stumbling upon this space might find some of the whakaaro helpful or interesting. If not, kei te pai.

This blog most importantly is a personal diary and a place where I can extract and unravel some of the thoughts running through my head on topics that are either important and/or interesting to me. It gives me a medium to bring order and clarity to some of my thoughts around modern technology and te ao Māori (recording them for later review).

Maybe my children will find the korero interesting to read when they're older...

Ngā tau iwa tekau

He tamaiti ahau nō ngā tau iwa tekau (I'm a child of the 90's).

I remember when I first played Super Mario Brothers and Duck Hunt on Nintendo. Alex the Kid and Sonic the Hedgehog on the Megadrive. Street Fighter 2 arcade battles at the local fish and chips shop. Installing Mortal Kombat on our IBM in MS-DOS. Playing Duke Nukem 3D over 28.8kbps over the network with friends. Playing Quake capture the flag online. Getting Final Fantasy VII for Christmas and clocking up over 100 hours on it.

It was a great decade to grow up in. Being around at the start of the gaming boom and witnessing the evolution of tech. I've got tons of fond memories of this period.

But the best part was being on the internet and the World Wide Web.

Kaihanga pūmanawa rorohiko

For around 14 years now I've been working professionally as a software developer. Mostly on the web and most of the time leaning towards the frontend of the stack (frontend applications, libraries and developer tooling). I also tinker with backend services and infrastructure (DevOps) from time to time. But my main interests are in UX engineering: designing and developing performant, accessible and intuitive user experiences.

Solving technical problems through programming is nice but it's only one part of what I enjoy about the work. I try to approach my work from the mindset of a systems thinker. Engineering interactive experiences and thinking deeply about user experience while maitaining a high-level view of all of the interconnected systems is an engaging and fulfilling process. I like to understand how everything under the hood works and how it all connects together.

I'm a problem solver and tinkerer and I've always been curious about how things work at a deep level. I enjoy intellectual challenges. Steep learning curves and deep layers of complexity are engaging and fun. The harder the problem, the better. Making sense of complex problems and simplifying complexity as much as possible keeps me engaged and curious.

This is where the craft of engineering enters the picture. Some don't see software development as a craft. I'm not one of them. I view software development through the lens of a craftsman. Like a sculptor drawing out elegant solutions from raw and rough material.

The experience of taking complex problems and simplifying them is like tuning channels through VHF/UHF band TV's. Tuning the reception until we get into the frequency range of a TV channel. Then proceeding to improve the picture by tweaking the tune to get close as possible to the optimum setting. Now the picture is as clear as possible. It's a process and experience of moving from ambiguity to clarity while also attempting to apply order to chaos. In my opinion this is the space where elegant solutions emerge.
